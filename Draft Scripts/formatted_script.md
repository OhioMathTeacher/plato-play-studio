[[[Plato: Allegory of the Cave - One-Act Draft]]]

[[Combined from group drafts. Edit after table read.]]

[[[Triad A - Scene 1: The Cave + Liberation]]]

[[Prologue: The Dialogue Begins]]

(The stage is dim. A faint, flickering amber light suggests firelight. Shadows move softly across a rough stone backdrop. SOCRATES I stands slightly apart, calm and composed, while GLAUCON I (GLAW-kon) listens nearby.)

SOCRATES I: Next, said I, compare our nature in respect of education and its lack to such an experience as this.

[[PART ONE: SETTING THE SCENE: THE CAVE AND THE FIRE]]

[The cave]

(Lights shift to reveal a dark cave interior. Several PRISONERS sit or kneel in a row, facing a blank wall. Their legs and necks are loosely bound with visible restraints. They remain completely still, unable to turn their heads. The entrance to the cave is suggested by a faint glow far upstage.)

SOCRATES I: Imagine this: People live under the earth in a cavelike dwelling. Stretching a long way up toward the daylight is its entrance, toward which the entire cave is gathered. The people have been in this dwelling since childhood, shackled by the legs and neck. Thus they stay in the same place so that there is only one thing for them to look at: whatever they encounter in front of their faces. But because they are shackled, they are unable to turn their heads around.

[A fire is behind them, and there is a sheet between the fire and the prisoners]

(A stronger flickering light ignites behind the prisoners, representing the fire. A raised walkway appears behind them. A low wall runs along it, partially blocking the view of figures moving behind it.)

SOCRATES I: Some light, of course, is allowed them, namely from a fire that casts its glow toward them from behind them, being above and at some distance. Between the fire and those who are shackled there runs a walkway at a certain height. Imagine that a low wall has been built the length of the walkway, like the low curtain that puppeteers put up, over which they show their puppets.

[The images carried before the fire]

(Behind a white sheet stretched across the raised walkway, SHADOW PUPPET OPERATORS use flashlights and cutouts to create moving silhouettes. In front of the sheet, FIGURES walk along the walkway carrying statues, cutouts, and objects—animals, people, and abstract shapes—held above the low wall so their shadows project clearly onto the cave wall in front of the prisoners. Some FIGURES speak softly; others remain silent.)

SOCRATES I: So now imagine that all along this low wall people are carrying all sorts of things that reach up higher than the wall: statues and other carvings made of stone or wood and many other artifacts that people have made. As you would expect, some are talking to each other and some are silent.

(GLAUCON steps slightly forward, studying the scene with curiosity and confusion.)

GLAUCON I: This is an unusual picture that you are presenting here, and these are unusual prisoners.

(SOCRATES turns slightly toward him, calm and assured.)

SOCRATES I: They are very much like us humans, I responded.

[What the prisoners see and hear]

(The shadows on the wall grow more pronounced. The prisoners lean slightly forward, focused entirely on the moving silhouettes. One or two react subtly, as if recognizing patterns or shapes.)

SOCRATES I: What do you think? From the beginning people like this have never managed, whether on their own or with the help of others, to see anything besides the shadows that are continually projected on the wall opposite them by the glow of the fire.

(GLAUCON gestures toward the prisoners' restraints.)

GLAUCON I: How could it be otherwise, since they are forced to keep their heads immobile for their entire lives?

SOCRATES I: And what do they see of the things that are being carried along? Do they not see simply these—namely the shadows?

GLAUCON I: Certainly.

(A faint echo effect is introduced. When the FIGURES behind the wall speak, their voices bounce and distort slightly, seeming to come from the wall itself. The prisoners react as if the shadows are speaking.)

SOCRATES I: Now if they were able to say something about what they saw and to talk it over, do you not think that they would regard that which they saw on the wall as beings?

GLAUCON I: They would have to.

(A FIGURE behind the wall speaks audibly. The echo makes it seem as though the sound comes from the shadow. A prisoner subtly reacts, as if attributing the voice to the shadow.)

SOCRATES I: And now what if this prison also had an echo reverberating off the wall in front of them? Whenever one of the people walking behind those in chains would make a sound, do you think the prisoners would imagine that the speaker were anyone other than the shadow passing in front of them?

GLAUCON I: Nothing else, by Zeus!

(The shadows continue moving rhythmically. The prisoners remain fixated, fully absorbed, unaware of anything beyond what they see.)

SOCRATES I: All in all, I responded, those who were chained would consider nothing besides the shadows of the artifacts as the unhidden.

GLAUCON I: That would absolutely have to be.

(Lights slowly dim further, leaving only the shadows flickering on the wall as the scene closes.)

[[[Triad B - Scene 2: The Ascent]]]

[The Release]

(GUARD 1 comes and releases one prisoner, THEA, from her shackles. SOCRATES II continues the narration.)

SOCRATES II: The prisoner has been freed from their imprisonment, and therefore their lack of knowledge.

(GUARD 1 forces THEA to stand up and look at the fire. THEA shields her eyes from the light with her hands.)

SOCRATES II: (While THEA is avoiding the light) The prisoner cannot look at the light without pain because she was only able to look at the shadows before.

GUARD 1: Just wait until you get out of the cave; these shadows don't really do real objects justice. You'll see, thinking shadows are real things is actually wrong!

GUARD 2: Hey, prisoner, see that, there? Do you know what that is?

(GUARD 2 points at an object that was casting a shadow in part 1)

THEA: What is that supposed to be? I have no idea what that is. (Turns back to shadows) I only know what these are. (Points to shadows)

[[FREEDOM, STAGE TWO]]

(GUARD 1 pulls THEA out of the cave into the bright light of outside. THEA tries to hide herself from the light, covering her eyes with her hands and gasping from pain.)

THEA: What is this terrible light? It's too bright! I can't see!

SOCRATES II: Because the former prisoner is blinded by the light, she can't see anything around her and realize that there are real objects around her.

(THEA starts to remove her hands from her face, squinting into the light.)

SOCRATES II: Over time, the former prisoner slowly gets used to the light. First, she looks at shadows, and then she looks at the reflection of objects in water. Eventually, she can see the objects around her as they are, and not just their shadows.

THEA: (Points to a chair) I see now, this is a chair! (Points to shadow of chair) This is merely a shadow.

[[FREEDOM, STAGE THREE: THE SUN]]

(The stage shifts from darkness to a gradual, warm light. THEA stands at the cave entrance, looking outward and shielding her eyes at first. Deeper in the cave, the prisoners remain dimly lit.)

SOCRATES II: (calmly) At last, I think, he would be able to look upon the sun itself—not merely its reflection in water or in some other place—but the sun as it truly is, in its own place, and to understand what it is.

GLAUCON II: (in quiet realization) Yes… it would have to happen that way. After all she has seen, she would finally be able to face it directly—to see it not as an image, but as truth itself.

(THEA slowly lifts her gaze upward, no longer shielding her eyes.)

SOCRATES II: And having done so, he would come to understand that the sun is what gives us seasons and years… that it governs everything visible in the world of light… and that it is, in some way, the cause of all those things the prisoners once saw as shadows.

GLAUCON II: (more confident) Yes, it would become clear to her. Only after leaving behind the shadows and reflections could she truly understand the source of everything she once believed was real.

(THEA looks back toward the cave.)

SOCRATES II: (reflective) And when she remembers her first home—the cave—and the kind of "knowledge" that passed for truth there… and the people she was once chained beside… do you think she would consider herself fortunate?

GLAUCON II: (softened tone) Very much so. She would see how much she has changed… and she would feel pity for those still trapped, believing in shadows as if they were real.

(Deeper in the cave, the prisoners continue watching shadows, unaware.)

SOCRATES II: But consider this—what if, in the cave, honors were given to those who were best at recognizing the shadows… remembering their patterns… and predicting what would come next?

GLAUCON II: (slightly unsettled) Then those honors would mean nothing to her now. What once seemed like wisdom would appear small and limited—only a skill within illusion.

SOCRATES II: (more intense) Would she envy those who are praised and powerful in the cave? Would she wish to return and compete among them?

GLAUCON II: (firm and emotional) No… I think she would rather endure anything—any hardship—than go back to living that way. Even the lowest life in the world above would be better than returning to false belief and becoming that kind of person again.

(THEA turns fully away from the cave, facing the cave entrance.)

[[[Triad C - Scene 3: The Return]]]

(A dark cave. Several prisoners sit chained, facing a wall. THEA slowly enters, stumbling and shielding her eyes. The prisoners turn and react silently, pointing, laughing, and recoiling. SOCRATES III and GLAUCON III observe and narrate.)

SOCRATES III: (thoughtfully) Now consider this, Glaucon. If the one who escaped were to return and sit again in her old place, would she not, coming suddenly from the sunlight, find her eyes filled with darkness?

GLAUCON III: (reflective) Yes… I think she would struggle greatly. After seeing clearly in the light, returning to darkness would make everything seem unclear again, almost as if she had lost her sight entirely.

(THEA struggles to see, reaching toward the shadows. The prisoners gesture mockingly.)

SOCRATES III: And if she were forced to engage again with those who remained, arguing about the shadows while her eyes are still weak, would she not be exposed to ridicule?

GLAUCON III: She would, and they would not understand why. To them, she would seem confused or mistaken, even though she has actually seen more than they have. Her weakness would look like ignorance instead of growth.

(The prisoners laugh silently, pointing at the Freed Prisoner. One mimics blindness.)

SOCRATES III: Would they not say that she went up only to return with her sight ruined, and that it is not worth going up at all?

GLAUCON III: (nodding) Yes, they would see her condition as proof that leaving the cave is harmful. They would believe that staying where they are, comfortable and familiar, is better than risking the unknown.

(THEA tries to gesture toward the cave entrance, urging them to follow. The prisoners recoil, shaking their heads.)

SOCRATES III: (more serious) And if she attempted to free them… to lead them upward into the light, would they not resist her?

GLAUCON III: (more somber) They would resist her strongly. They would be afraid of what they do not understand, and they would cling to what feels safe, even if it is only an illusion.

SOCRATES III: (heavy, serious) And if, in their fear and anger, they were able to seize her, this one who tries to lead them out of the only world they have ever known, would they not, seeing her as a threat to everything they believe, go so far as to kill her?

GLAUCON III: (quiet, heavy) Yes… I believe they would. If her words threatened everything they believed to be true, they would see her not as a guide, but as a danger.

(The prisoners surround THEA. Lights slowly dim to black.)
