Today's Goals: Discover Plato as a mathematician through hands-on exploration of Platonic solids, understand why there are exactly 5 platonic solids (and no more), and preview the allegory of the cave through ted-ed video
1. Setting the Stage (10 minutes: 8:30-8:40)

Introduction (5 min)

  • Devices away. Hands-on only today.
  • Today we meet Plato—a Greek philosopher from about 2,400 years ago.
  • Legend says above the entrance to his Academy was inscribed: 'Let no one ignorant of geometry enter.'
  • Plato believed mathematics revealed deep truths about reality. Let's explore one of his mathematical ideas.

Definitions (5 minutes)

  • A Polyhedron is a 3D shape where:
    • Every face is a flat polygon (like a triangle, square, or pentagon).
    • Every edge connects exactly two faces, forming a closed solid with no gaps or holes.
    • Think of it as the general family of all straight-edged, flat-faced 3D objects—from a simple pyramid to a complex diamond.
  • A Platonic solid is a 3D shape where:
    • Every face is the same regular polygon
    • The same number of faces meet at every vertex (corner)
    • Think of it as the most 'perfect' or 'symmetric' 3D shapes possible.

Show Polydrons: You can build with these—equilateral triangles, squares, and pentagons.

2. Platonic Solids Challenge (30 minutes: 8:40-9:10)

The Challenge (20 min)

  • Form small groups (2-3 students)
  • Using ONLY equilateral triangles or squares, how many different Platonic solids can you build?
  • Using a mix of polydron pieces construct various types of polyedra.
  • For both general polyedra and Platonic solids, record the number of Vertices, Faces, and Edges on the provided chart.
  • For Platonic Solids, consider the following:
    • How many triangles meet at each corner of that shape?
    • What happens if you try to put 6 triangles at a vertex?
    • Can you close it up into a solid?
  • For Polyhedra, consider the following:
    • Can you make a polyhedron with a "dent" in it?
    • Can you make a polyhedron with more edges than faces? If so, do it!
    • Can you find a relationship between Faces, Vertices, and Edges? If so, what is the relationship?

Share & Discuss (10 min)

Think-Pair-Share:

  • What shapes did your group build?
  • What patterns did you uncover? 
  • How many Platonic solids did you build?
